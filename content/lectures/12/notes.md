# Lecture 12: Inferring Ancient Environments: Paleogeography

Source PDF: `source.pdf`  
Pages: 6  
Exam priority: High

## Role In The Course

Explains how ancient continental positions and environments are reconstructed.

## Lecture Goals

- Know how ancient continental positions are reconstructed.
- Understand paleogeographic maps as interpretations.
- Use reconstructions to think about past and future Earth.

## High-Yield Content

- Continents move, so ancient environments must be interpreted in ancient geographic context.
- Evidence includes paleomagnetism, fossils, rock belts, glacial deposits, mountain belts, and seafloor/ocean-basin history.
- Paleogeographic reconstructions affect climate, circulation, sea level, habitat area, and isolation/connectivity.
- Future Earth is an extension of the same plate-motion logic.
- Paleogeography is central to extinction and radiation explanations.

## Study Targets

- Be able to say why a rock found at one modern latitude may have formed at another.
- Know how continental positions can create glaciation, aridity, seaways, and epicontinental seas.
- Use maps actively, not as decoration.

## Active Recall

- What evidence can place continents in ancient positions?
- How does paleogeography alter climate and marine habitats?
- Why does supercontinent assembly matter for life?
