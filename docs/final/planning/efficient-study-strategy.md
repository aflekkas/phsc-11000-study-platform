# Efficient Final Study Strategy

This strategy uses the local syllabus, Canvas announcements, lecture PDFs, lab PDFs, and public course-history research.

## Bottom Line

The efficient path is not rereading all PDFs front to back. The efficient path is:

1. Build one master timeline.
2. Build one mechanism/evidence table.
3. Drill the lecture goals.
4. Use labs as applied examples.
5. Practice short written explanations.

## Why This Is The Right Strategy

- The final exam is categorized as lecture material in the course-details PDF.
- Canvas announcements say quizzes primarily cover lecture material.

Use this if you have one focused evening.

Write the 20-event timeline from `course-goals.md` without notes. Check it. Rewrite it.

- Evidence type.
- What it tells you.

Include:

- Sedimentary structures.
- Fossils.
- Stratigraphic order.
- Radiometric dating.
- Stable isotopes.
- Paleogeographic maps.
- Diversity curves.
- Carbon-cycle records.

### Hour 3: Tier 1 Lectures

Read the markdown notes for L10, L11, L13, L15, L17, L20, L21, L22.

For each, answer:

- Main event/process?
- Evidence?
- Cause?
- Consequence?
- One connection?

### Hour 4: Labs As Examples

Skim:

- Lab 5 for sedimentology/environment inference.
- Lab 6 for Cambrian Radiation.
- Lab 7 for Carboniferous/Paleozoic communities.
- Lab 8 for Mesozoic Marine Revolution.

Do not redo every lab question. Pull examples and diagrams.

### Hour 5: Compare/Contrast Practice

Write bullet answers for:

- Cambrian Radiation vs. Mesozoic Marine Revolution.
- Paleozoic marine communities vs. post-Paleozoic marine communities.
- End-Permian vs. end-Cretaceous extinction.
- Natural carbon-cycle/climate change vs. human climate change.
- Oxygenation vs. Snowball Earth as Earth-system feedback stories.

### Hour 6: Active Recall

Use `../lecture-content-index.md`. Open each lecture note and answer the active-recall questions out loud or in rough notes. Mark weak lectures red.

## Practice Prompts

Use these as final-style rehearsal.

1. Explain how sedimentary structures can be used to infer depositional environment.
2. Explain how stable isotopes can act as environmental proxies.
3. Explain why oxygen production and oxygen accumulation are not the same.
4. Explain the Snowball Earth hypothesis as a feedback story.
5. Compare the Cambrian Radiation and Ordovician Radiation.
6. Explain how land plants altered Earth surface environments.
7. Explain the causes and consequences of the end-Permian extinction.
8. Explain what the Mesozoic Marine Revolution changed.
9. Compare end-Permian and end-Cretaceous extinction mechanisms.
10. Explain Cenozoic cooling using tectonics, CO2, ocean gateways, and feedbacks.
11. Explain how humans affect biological communities.
12. Explain how fossil fuel burning connects Carboniferous coal swamps to modern climate change.

## Rule For Studying Any Lecture

Do not leave a lecture until you can say:

- "The main thing is..."
- "The evidence is..."
- "The mechanism is..."
- "The consequence is..."
- "This connects to..."

If you can do that for all 27 lectures, you are studying the right way.
