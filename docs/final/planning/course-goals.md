# Course Goals

This file turns the syllabus, Canvas announcements, and lecture PDFs into a study-facing goal map.

## What The Course Is Trying To Teach

PHSC 11000 is not mainly a memorization course, even though there are many names and events. The central skill is explaining how Earth surface environments changed through time, using geological evidence and Earth-system mechanisms.

By the final, the target is to be able to do five things:

1. Explain the physical controls on environments.
2. Explain the biological controls on environments.
3. Infer ancient environments from evidence.
4. Place major Earth-history transitions in order.
5. Connect past environmental change to human-driven change.

## The Five Repeating Question Types

Most final prep can be reduced to these:

1. What changed?
2. What caused it?
3. What evidence shows it?
4. What feedbacks amplified, reduced, or complicated it?
5. What were the biological/environmental consequences?

Use those five questions for every major event.

## The Course Spine

### 1. Present Environmental Controls

Lectures: L1-L6.

Main goal: understand the controls that produce environments today.

- Energy input and insolation.
- Atmosphere and greenhouse effect.
- Oceans and heat/material circulation.
- Plate tectonics and topography.
- Organisms as ecosystem engineers.

Final use: these are the mechanisms you use later to explain deep-time events.

### 2. Geological Inference Tools

Lectures: L7-L13.

Main goal: understand how geologists know what ancient environments were like.

- Rocks record formation conditions.
- Stratigraphy orders events.
- Geologic time gives scale and chronology.
- Sedimentology identifies depositional environments.
- Stable isotopes act as proxies for climate and carbon-cycle conditions.
- Paleogeography reconstructs ancient continental/ocean positions.
- Earth-system science ties evidence and mechanisms into models.

Final use: this is the evidence toolkit. If a question asks "how do we know," answer from this block.

### 3. Major Deep-Time Transitions

Lectures: L14-L24.

Main goal: know the big Earth-history changes in cause/evidence/consequence form.

- Early Earth and origin of life.
- Oxygenic photosynthesis and rise of oxygen.
- Snowball Earth.
- Cambrian Radiation.
- Colonization of land.
- Carboniferous coal swamps.
- Paleozoic radiations and extinctions.
- End-Permian extinction.
- Mesozoic Marine Revolution.
- End-Cretaceous extinction.
- Cenozoic cooling, mammals, and ice ages.

Final use: this is the main content body.

### 4. Humans As Earth-System Agents

Lectures: L25-L27.

Main goal: connect human evolution and human impacts back to the course mechanisms.

- Humans evolved within Cenozoic environmental change.
- Humans alter biological communities through overkill, land use, introductions, and conservation choices.
- Humans alter climate through fossil carbon, greenhouse gases, land use, and feedbacks.

Final use: do not study human impacts as a detached ending. They reuse the same climate, carbon-cycle, extinction, and ecosystem-engineering concepts from earlier lectures.

## Highest-Yield Cross-Course Themes

- Climate is controlled by energy input, greenhouse gases, albedo, oceans, geography, and feedbacks.
- Sea level changes habitat area and can drive ecological turnover.
- Plate tectonics controls topography, paleogeography, volcanism, ocean circulation, and long-term climate.
- Life changes environments: photosynthesis, reefs, burrowing, land plants, coal swamps, humans.
- Mass extinctions are environmental crises plus ecological selectivity.
- Radiations often follow ecological opportunity, new traits, or environmental reset.
- Evidence is indirect: rocks, fossils, isotopes, stratigraphy, maps, and models have to be interpreted.

## What To Memorize Vs. What To Explain

Memorize:

- Timeline order.
- Lecture titles and major events.
- Major extinction names and approximate placement.
- Key terms: insolation, albedo, greenhouse effect, stratigraphy, isotope fractionation, paleogeography, ecosystem engineering, anoxia, durophagy, mass extinction, convergent evolution.

Explain:

- Mechanisms.
- Evidence chains.
- Feedback loops.
- Why one event matters for later events.
- Similarities/differences between transitions.

If time is limited, explanation beats detail memorization.
