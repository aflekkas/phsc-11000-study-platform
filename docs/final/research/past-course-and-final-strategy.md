## What Seems Stable Across Offerings

### Stable Official Course Identity

The official catalog frames PHSC 11000 as a geoscience Physical Sciences Core course about how physical and biological processes determine surface environments. The Spring/Webster version emphasizes environmental and life changes over roughly 4.5 billion years, methods of geological inference, origin of life, oxygenic photosynthesis, origin of animals, mass extinctions, ecosystem evolution, and human environmental effects.

That matches the current lecture sequence almost exactly.

### Stable Spring/Webster Shape

The public traces from 2018, 2020, 2022, 2024, 2025, and 2026 point to a stable structure:

- Lecture notes with explicit lecture goals.
- No textbook.
- Labs involving rocks, fossils, datasets, and applied interpretation.
- A cumulative Earth-history arc.
- Heavy use of geological inference.
- Lab themes that recur across years.

The pandemic Spring 2020 version was asynchronous and had unusual final logistics, so do not use it as a model for the 2026 final format.

### Stable Lab Themes

Past and current lab traces line up:

- Plate tectonics.
- Rocks.
- Inferring ancient environments.
- Sedimentology.
- Cambrian Radiation.
- Carboniferous/Paleozoic communities.
- Mesozoic Marine Revolution.
- Marine reptiles.
- Diversity curves.
- Convergent evolution.

This matters because labs show the examples instructors expect students to apply.

## Expected Final Content

This is inference from current materials plus past-course continuity.

### Directly Supported

- The final is 30% of the course grade.
- The final is attached to lecture material.
- Quizzes primarily covered lecture material.
- Labs reinforce lecture learning and apply concepts.
- Current lecture PDFs cover all major course goals.

### Strong Inference

The final is likely cumulative and conceptual. It probably asks for:

- Definitions plus application.
- Event sequence/timeline reasoning.
- Cause/evidence/consequence explanations.
- Interpretation of diagrams or evidence types.
- Compare/contrast between major transitions.
- Short-answer style mechanism explanations.

## What Past Student Signals Mean

Public student comments are weak evidence, but they are useful for strategy. The pattern is:

- The course is not described as mathematically hard.
- It can feel memorization-heavy.
- Labs can be long and technical.
- Keeping up with lecture notes matters.

Best interpretation: the final is probably not about difficult calculations. The risk is confusing many similar events, evidence types, and mechanisms. Study by organizing, not by rereading.

## Efficient Final Strategy

### 1. Timeline First

Make the timeline automatic. If the order is shaky, every explanation becomes harder.

Minimum order:

Early Earth -> origin of life -> oxygenation -> Snowball Earth -> Cambrian Radiation -> Ordovician Radiation -> terrestrialization -> coal swamps -> end-Permian -> Mesozoic Marine Revolution -> end-Cretaceous -> Cenozoic cooling -> humans.

### 2. Evidence Toolkit Second

For each evidence type, know what it can and cannot tell you.

- Sedimentology: depositional environment.
- Stratigraphy: relative sequence.
- Radiometric dating: absolute age.
- Fossils: age, environment, ecology, evolution.
- Isotopes: climate, temperature, ice volume, carbon cycle.
- Paleogeography: continental position, ocean gateways, climate context.
- Diversity curves: origination, extinction, recovery, ecological shifts.

### 3. Events In Cause/Evidence/Consequence Form

For each major event:

- Cause/mechanism.
- Evidence.
- Consequence.
- Feedback.
- Lecture/lab example.

### 4. Compare/Contrast

Likely high-yield comparisons:

- Cambrian Radiation vs. Mesozoic Marine Revolution.
- End-Permian vs. end-Cretaceous extinction.
- Paleozoic vs. post-Paleozoic marine communities.
- Oxygenation vs. Snowball Earth as Earth-system transitions.
- Natural deep-time climate change vs. anthropogenic climate change.
- Land plants vs. humans as ecosystem engineers.

### 5. Figures Last

After you know the story, review figures. Do not start by passively staring at figures.

Targets:

- Energy budget.
- Atmospheric/ocean circulation.
- Plate boundaries.
- Rock cycle.
- Stratigraphic diagrams.
- Timescale.
- Sedimentary structures.
- Isotope curves.
- Carbon cycle.
- Oxygen and CO2 curves.
- Extinction/diversity curves.
- MMR community diagrams.
- Cenozoic climate curves.
- Human emissions/climate projections.
